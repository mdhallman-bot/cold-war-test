📖 Key Vocabulary — Use these definitions to help you answer the questions.

Communism:a system where the government controls the economy and there is no private property
Capitalism:a system where individuals and businesses own property and control trade
Containment:the U.S. policy of stopping the spread of communism to other countries
Cold War:a period of tension between the U.S. and Soviet Union (1945–1991); no direct fighting between them
Superpower:a nation with great military and economic power that influences world events
Arms Race:the competition between the U.S. and Soviet Union to build more and stronger weapons
Domino Theory:the idea that if one country became communist, neighboring countries would follow, like falling dominoes
Deterrence:building up weapons so the enemy is afraid to attack
Blockade:using military force to cut off supplies or movement to a place
Proxy War:a conflict where two superpowers support opposite sides instead of fighting each other directly
Negotiation:talking and working together to reach an agreement without fighting
Escalation:when a conflict gets bigger or more intense over time
Section A
Origins & Containment
Source A President Harry S. Truman addressing Congress, March 12, 1947
Background: After World War II ended in 1945, the United States and the Soviet Union became rivals. The U.S. had a democratic, capitalist system. The Soviet Union had a communist system. Both countries had nuclear weapons. Communist forces were threatening the governments of Greece and Turkey. President Truman asked Congress for money and military support to help those countries fight communism.
[Photo: President Truman addressing a joint session of Congress, March 12, 1947 — Truman Library & Museum]
President Harry S. Truman addressing a joint session of Congress, asking for $400 million in aid to Greece and Turkey. Truman Library & Museum.

"At the present moment in world history, nearly every nation must choose between alternative ways of life… One way of life is based upon the will of the majority… The second way of life is based upon the will of a minority forcibly imposed upon the majority… I believe that it must be the policy of the United States to support free peoples who are resisting attempted subjugation by armed minorities or by outside pressures."

Section B
The Korean War
Source B General Douglas MacArthur, Letter to House Republican Leader Joseph Martin, March 20, 1951
Background: After WWII, Korea was divided — North Korea became communist (supported by the Soviet Union and China), and South Korea was supported by the United States. In 1950, North Korea invaded South Korea, starting the Korean War. General MacArthur was the U.S. commander. He disagreed with President Truman's plan to fight a "limited war." MacArthur wanted to attack communist China directly. Truman later fired MacArthur for publicly disagreeing with U.S. policy.
"It seems strangely difficult for some to realize that here in Asia is where the Communist conspirators have elected to make their play for global conquest… We must win. There is no substitute for victory."

Section C
Cuban Missile Crisis
Source C President John F. Kennedy, Television Address to the Nation, October 22, 1962
Background: In October 1962, U.S. spy planes discovered that the Soviet Union was building nuclear missile bases in Cuba — just 90 miles from Florida. President Kennedy demanded the missiles be removed. For 13 days, the U.S. and Soviet Union were on the edge of nuclear war. The crisis ended when the Soviets agreed to remove the missiles in exchange for a U.S. promise not to invade Cuba.
"The 1930s taught us a clear lesson: aggressive conduct, if allowed to go unchecked and unchallenged, ultimately leads to war… Our goal is not the victory of might, but the vindication of right — not peace at the expense of freedom, but both peace and freedom, here in this hemisphere and, we hope, around the world."

Section E
Vietnam War & Domino Theory
Source F President Dwight D. Eisenhower, Press Conference, April 7, 1954
Background: After the Korean War, the U.S. became increasingly involved in Vietnam. Vietnam was divided — communist North Vietnam was supported by the Soviet Union and China, and South Vietnam was supported by the U.S. President Eisenhower introduced the domino theory to explain why the U.S. needed to prevent communism from spreading in Southeast Asia.
"You have a row of dominoes set up, you knock over the first one, and what will happen to the last one is the certainty that it will go over very quickly… Asia… has already lost some 450 million of its peoples to the Communist dictatorship, and we simply can't afford greater losses."
Source G Political Cartoon: "…And of course, if Cambodia fell, then Laos would fall, and if Laos fell…" — Doug Marlette, c. 1972, Library of Congress
What to look for: This cartoon shows President Nixon and a military officer walking past a graveyard. The gravestones are shaped like dominoes and are labeled "Viet War Dead." The officer is describing the domino theory, explaining why the U.S. had to keep fighting. Think about what message the cartoonist is sending about the domino theory.
